note from me (Thomas), when there's a reference to G.E. Moore below, it's because On Certainty, the book this idea originates from, starts off with a couple manuscripts that refer to Moore's argument. Moore claimed he could *know*, for certain, that an external world exists, just by holding up his hand and saying "here is a hand." Wittgenstein doesn't think that's wrong exactly, he thinks it's the word "know" that's doing something weird there, and he uses Moore's example in his opening manuscripts to show us the puzzle, before eventually giving the 'solution', which is hinge propositions.

# Hinge Propositions

*From Wittgenstein's last notebooks, published as* On Certainty *(1969). All § numbers refer to its remarks.*

**1.** A door can open only because its hinges stay put. Wittgenstein turns this into a picture of the mind: "the *questions* that we raise and our *doubts* depend on the fact that some propositions are exempt from doubt, are as it were like hinges on which those turn" (§341). "If I want the door to turn, the hinges must stay put" (§343).

**2.** Your hinges, right now: *I have two hands. The world existed long before my birth. Things don't vanish when I look away. My name is what I think it is.* Nobody taught you these. Nobody proved them to you. You have never checked a single one — and everything you *have* ever checked was checked against them.

**3.** The occasion: G.E. Moore had raised a hand before an audience — *Here is one hand. I know this; therefore an external world exists.* Wittgenstein's reply: Moore is right that this cannot be doubted, and wrong that he *knows* it.

**4.** "I know" earns its keep where checking makes sense: *I know the train leaves at six* — I can look it up, I could be wrong. But how would you check that you have hands? With your eyes? Why would the eyes be surer than the hands? Where nothing counts as finding out, "I know" is an idle wheel.

**5.** So a hinge is not the best-proven of our propositions. It is not proven at all. It is not a picture hanging in the frame — it is the frame. All testing already happens inside a system, and the system is "not so much the point of departure, as the element in which arguments have their life" (§105).

**6.** Doubt is no exception. To doubt your hands you must trust your eyes, your words, your memory of what "hand" means — hinges all the way down. "If you tried to doubt everything you would not get as far as doubting anything. The game of doubting itself presupposes certainty" (§115).

**7.** This is his answer to the skeptic — not a refutation, a dissolution. Descartes' doubt isn't *false*; it is idle. A doubt that doubted everything would not be a doubt (§450). The skeptic's words only mean something while the hinges hold: he saws the branch he sits on.

**8.** Where does this certainty come from, if not evidence? From *action*. A child is never told "the world exists" — it learns to reach, fetch, ask, and the hinges are swallowed down with everything else. "Light dawns gradually over the whole" (§141). "It is our *acting* which lies at the bottom of the language-game" (§204).

**9.** But hinges are not eternal axioms. Picture a river: the moving water is inquiry; the bed is the hardened propositions that channel it. "The river-bed of thoughts may shift" (§97) — sand washes out here, hardens there. One age's bedrock is another's current.

**10.** His own example of bedrock, written around 1950: *no one has ever been on the moon.* In 1969 — the very year *On Certainty* was published — the bed shifted under that sentence. The metaphor had already made room for it.

**11.** Notice what failing a hinge looks like. Miscounting your change is a mistake — a wrong move inside the game. Sincerely doubting you have a body is not playing badly; it is falling out of the game ("I should take him to be a half-wit," §257). Losing a hinge is not losing a belief. It is losing the ability to believe, doubt, or mean anything in that region.

**12.** At the bottom, then, no floor. "The difficulty is to realize the groundlessness of our believing" (§166). Not irrational — *before* rationality: the place where reasons run out and only life stands. "It is there — like our life" (§559).

---

**In one breath:** a hinge proposition is a certainty you never learned and could never prove, because proving, doubting, and knowing only work while it stands fast — the stillness that lets the door swing.

---

*Wittgenstein wrote the last of these remarks two days before his death in April 1951. The idea now runs under the name **hinge epistemology** (Moyal-Sharrock, Pritchard, Coliva), a leading contemporary answer to skepticism.*
