## Quick notes — what the clip says

> [!summary] In one line
> Alex O'Connor uses **reflective equilibrium** to describe the two-way "tug and tussle" between your *moral principles* and the *conclusions* they spit out — when a principle leads somewhere absurd, you're allowed to revise the principle rather than just swallow the conclusion.

**The setup — how do we handle moral disagreement?**
- Beyond just arguing on TV etc., there's a "wonderful concept": **reflective equilibrium**.
- He credits it to **John Rawls**, from his work on how we should organize society.

**The "big ethics machine" analogy**
- Picture it as a machine: you feed in your **moral principles**, and out come the **consequences** they cause.
- Example — **Peter Singer's** idea: *suffering matters no matter who's suffering.*
- Plug that principle in, and what does the machine output?
  - We catch ~**440 billion shrimp** every year.
  - For **$1** to the shrimp welfare project, you can save ~**1,500 of them** from being tortured before death.
  - So the machine concludes: donating there is a highly effective use of money.

**The pivotal question — is that the best use of your money?**
- *Maybe.* But this is where reflective equilibrium bites.
- It's a **two-way tug and tussle** between:
  1. Your **premises / principles**, and
  2. The **conclusions** they generate.
- The key question it governs: **how much stock do you place in whether that conclusion is true?**
  - Do I just *accept* the conclusion because my premises forced it on me?
  - Or — even though I hold those premises — if I *don't like the conclusion*, maybe I should doubt the premises instead.

**The upshot**
- You don't have to be a one-way slave to your premises. When an argument runs from plausible principles to a deeply counter-intuitive conclusion, reflective equilibrium says the *conclusion's* weirdness can count as evidence against a *premise*. You adjust back and forth until principles and judgments settle into balance ("equilibrium").
- Alex's closing beat: he's "not so sure" you must just accept a strongly unintuitive conclusion — "I think it's [a matter of] feeling" as much as pure logic. *(clip cuts off here)*
