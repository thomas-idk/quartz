# Anarchy Adjudicated: Realism, Liberalism, and the Point Where the Facts Run Out

## Abstract

The realism–liberalism debate in international relations has run for roughly eighty years without resolution, which is itself a datum requiring explanation. This paper attempts an adjudication conducted under explicit rules: arguments are reconstructed at full strength, deconstructed by the opposing side, and the exchange is continued until it terminates in one of three states — a settled fact, an open fact with a specified missing observation, or an identified hinge commitment that empirical evidence cannot reach. The commitment throughout is empirical-first: facts about the world are treated as the ultimate decider wherever they can decide, and the descent into logic and then into hinge epistemology is made only where the facts demonstrably run out, with the location of that boundary marked precisely rather than gestured at. The finding is threefold. First, both theories in their universal covering-law forms are empirically dead: each has suffered clean out-of-sample predictive failures against the other's successes, and each survives only by scope retreats that its critics reasonably describe as degenerative. Second, both survive robustly as conditional mechanism-bundles whose domains of operation can be partially specified — realist dynamics dominate as information, iteration, and enforcement go to zero and stakes go to the existential; liberal dynamics dominate in the opposite corner of the parameter space — and the historical record is best read as movement within that space rather than as the vindication of either corner. Third, after the empirical and formal layers have done all the work they can do, a residue remains that is genuinely undecidable by facts: a choice of decision rule under irreducible uncertainty about other states' intentions, under fat-tailed loss distributions, in a domain where beliefs feed back into the reality they describe. That residue is narrow, it is precisely locatable, and it has the structure of a hinge — but the paper closes by showing that even the hinge layer casts an empirical shadow, because hinges of international practice form and erode historically, and we can watch them do so.

## 1. Introduction: The Problem of Non-Resolution

Every discipline has debates that persist, but few persist like this one. Thucydides against the Athenian envoys' own logic, Hobbes against Locke, Carr against the interwar idealists, Waltz against Keohane, Mearsheimer against everyone: the same argument recurs across twenty-four centuries with updated vocabulary and undiminished confidence on both sides. In 1990, structural realists predicted that NATO would wither and Europe would return to multipolar rivalry; liberals predicted institutional deepening. In 2001, liberals bet that admitting China to the World Trade Organization would liberalize it; realists predicted a richer, harder rival. Both sides can now point to the other's failed prediction. Neither side has conceded anything.

Persistence of this kind admits three explanations. The first is that one side is simply right and the other is engaged in motivated reasoning — possible, but after eighty years of professionalized testing by thousands of scholars with every incentive to score a decisive kill, increasingly implausible as a complete account. The second is that the debate is empirically undecidable in principle, a clash of worldviews or temperaments dressed up as social science — the counsel of despair, and premature, because as this paper will show, large tracts of the debate *have* been decided by evidence, and the combatants have quietly abandoned positions their predecessors held. The third explanation, defended here, is that the debate as conventionally staged is ill-posed: it presents as a single binary question ("which theory is true?") what is in fact a layered structure of questions — some settled, some open but answerable, some open and starving for data, and a small residue that is not empirical at all. The debate does not resolve at the surface because the surface is not where the disagreement lives. The task is therefore not to declare a winner but to perform a controlled descent: to determine, at each layer, what the disagreement actually consists in, adjudicate what can be adjudicated at that layer, and carry only the genuine residue downward.

The method imposes four disciplines. First, *steelmanning*: each theory is reconstructed in its strongest available form before any deconstruction begins, because refuting the weak version of a research program is a rhetorical act, not an analytical one. Second, *register-tracking*: at every step it is marked whether a claim is descriptive (how states do behave), prescriptive (how they should), or constitutive (what the international system is), because — as Section 5.4 argues — unmarked switching between these registers is the perpetual-motion machine that keeps the debate running past its evidential fuel. Third, *rules of evidence stated in advance*: Section 4.0 specifies what a single historical case can and cannot establish, when one example suffices and when it is nearly weightless, before any case is examined — so that the weight assigned to evidence is fixed prior to knowing which side it favors. Fourth, a *stopping rule*: the deconstruction of any argument continues until it terminates in a settled fact, a specified missing fact, or an identified hinge; and the regress of skeptical challenges is halted at the point where further doubt would indict not international-relations theory specifically but observational science as such. That last clause matters. The hinges that make science reliable — the stability of the external world, the general trustworthiness of records and instruments, the legitimacy of induction — are taken as given here, and both theoretical camps stand on them equally. The question is whether, above that shared riverbed, the realist–liberal fork can be closed by facts, and if not, what exactly remains open.

## 2. The Theories at Full Strength

### 2.1 Realism's Engine

Realism at its strongest is not the cynic's shrug that "states do bad things." It is a deductive engine with a small number of premises and a claim of tragic necessity. In Mearsheimer's canonical formulation (2001), five assumptions do the work: the international system is anarchic, meaning there is no authority above states capable of enforcing agreements; all great powers possess some offensive military capability; no state can ever be certain of another state's intentions, now or in the future; survival is the primary goal of states; and states are rational actors. From these, the argument runs, fear follows — because in a self-help system, misplaced trust can be fatal and there is no appeal; from fear follows the accumulation of relative power as insurance; from universal insurance-seeking follows the security dilemma, in which one state's defensive measures are another's looming threat; and from the security dilemma follows the recurrence of arms competition, alliance politics, and war, regardless of the character, regime type, or intentions of the units. The system, not the units, produces the tragedy. This is why Waltz (1979) insisted that his was a theory of international politics and not of foreign policy: it predicts recurrent patterns — balancing, the formation and dissolution of alliances tracking threats, the punishment of states that ignore power realities — not the behavior of any particular state on any particular Tuesday.

The tradition contains real internal variation, and the strongest version borrows selectively. Classical realism (Morgenthau 1948; Niebuhr; and behind them Thucydides and Hobbes) grounds the engine in anthropology — an *animus dominandi*, a lust for power constitutive of political man. Defensive structural realism (Waltz 1979; Jervis 1978; Glaser 2010) grounds it purely in structure and holds that states seek security, not power maximization, so that moderate, satiable policies are often structurally rational. Offensive realism (Mearsheimer 2001) replies that because intentions are unknowable and the future is long, security itself demands maximal power, ideally regional hegemony. Hegemonic-stability theory (Gilpin 1981) redirects attention from balance to preponderance: order exists when a dominant power supplies it and decays as the dominant power declines. Neoclassical realism reintroduces domestic variables as transmission belts between structural pressure and state response. What unites them — the hard core, in Lakatos's (1970) sense — is the triad of anarchy, self-help, and the primacy of security competition among states-as-units, together with a characteristic epistemic posture: because betrayal is possible and catastrophic, prudence requires planning against capabilities rather than trusting professed intentions.

Realism at full strength also makes a claim about cooperation, and it is subtler than the caricature. Cooperation happens constantly, realists grant; the claim is that it is bounded and conditional. States worry not only about absolute gains from cooperation but about relative gains — whether the partner gains more — because today's partner is tomorrow's potential threat (Grieco 1988). Institutions exist, but as Mearsheimer (1994/95) argued in the sharpest statement of the position, they are epiphenomenal: reflections of the underlying distribution of power, created by the strong to serve the strong, complied with when convenient and ignored when core interests are at stake, and therefore possessing no independent causal force where it matters. The League of Nations watched Manchuria and Abyssinia; the United Nations Security Council is a great-power concert with a veto; the strong do what they can. Finally, realism claims a disciplining mechanism: the system *selects*. States that ignore power realities — that free-ride on parchment guarantees, that disarm on faith — are punished, diminished, or destroyed, and the survivors are socialized into realpolitik whatever their founding ideals (Waltz 1979). Poland partitioned three times is the standing exhibit.

### 2.2 Liberalism's Tripod

Liberalism at its strongest is likewise not the naïf's faith that "people are basically good." It is a claim about *variables*: that the war-generating frictions realism treats as constants — fear, uncertainty, the impossibility of enforcement — are in fact variable, that their values are causally influenced by identifiable features of states and their relations, and that those features are manipulable by design. The tradition's philosophical charter is Kant's *Perpetual Peace* (1795), which proposed three articles — republican constitutions, a pacific federation of such states, and cosmopolitan hospitality sustained by commerce — and, crucially, a mechanism for each. Republics are constrained because those who pay for war in blood and treasure hold a veto over those who declare it; federation substitutes law for the state of nature among the federated; and the spirit of commerce, incompatible with war, enlists self-interest on the side of peace. Modern liberal theory is best understood as the operationalization of Kant's tripod, one leg at a time, plus a fourth foundational move.

The first leg is *commercial liberalism*: economic interdependence raises the cost of war and creates domestic constituencies with a stake in peace (Angell 1910; Rosecrance 1986). Its sophisticated modern form, examined closely in Section 4.4, is Copeland's (1996; 2014) trade-expectations theory, in which what pacifies is not present trade but confident expectations of future trade. The second leg is *republican liberalism*, the democratic-peace research program: Doyle (1983) recovered Kant's claim and the striking empirical regularity that consolidated liberal democracies do not fight wars against one another, a regularity Levy (1988) called the closest thing to an empirical law that international relations possesses, and which Russett and Oneal (2001) embedded in a statistical framework testing all three Kantian legs simultaneously. The third leg is *institutionalism*, and its strongest form is deliberately built on realist ground: Keohane (1984) granted anarchy, granted state egoism, granted the absence of enforcement, and demonstrated — with the machinery of iterated games, following Axelrod (1984) — that self-interested states in anarchy still rationally construct institutions, because institutions reduce transaction costs, generate information, create reputational stakes, link issues so that defection in one domain is punished in another, and lengthen the shadow of the future. Institutions on this account are not world government and not parchment idealism; they are equilibrium-supporting technology. The rational-design literature (Koremenos, Lipson and Snidal 2001) extended the point: institutional features — escape clauses, monitoring provisions, membership rules — vary systematically with the cooperation problems they are engineered to solve, which is precisely what one would expect if institutions were causal instruments and not decorative reflections of power.

The fourth move is foundational. Moravcsik (1997) reformulated liberalism as a positive social-scientific theory whose bottom-most claim is that the fundamental actors in politics are individuals and private groups; that states are not billiard balls with a given "national interest" but transmission mechanisms whose preferences are constituted by whichever domestic coalition captures them; and that international politics is therefore the interaction of *state preferences*, with power mattering only within the space preferences define. This is the deep structural difference from realism: realism black-boxes the state and derives behavior from the system; liberalism opens the box and derives behavior from what the system's units want, which varies. Germany in 1914, 1938, and 1990 occupied approximately the same geographic position under the same anarchy; its behavior differed as its regime and ruling coalition differed. On the liberal account that variation is the explanandum realism cannot reach.

### 2.3 The Shared Premise and the Real Question

It is essential to notice, before the clash begins, what the strongest versions share: *anarchy*. Keohane's institutionalism assumes it; Moravcsik's liberalism does not deny it; the democratic-peace literature operates entirely within it. The debate is therefore not, despite a century of framing, about whether the international system lacks a common sovereign. It is about the *elasticity of anarchy's consequences*: whether anarchy has one logic (fear, self-help, recurrence) or many logics depending on the values of variables that anarchy itself does not fix — information, iteration, enforcement substitutes, regime type, economic structure, and ideas. Wendt's (1992) famous title states the liberal-adjacent limit position: anarchy is what states make of it. Mearsheimer's position is the opposite limit: anarchy makes states what they are. Everything that follows is an attempt to locate, empirically where possible and logically where necessary, where between those limits the world actually sits — and whether its position is fixed or moves.

## 3. The Deductive Clash: Does Either Theory Follow From Its Premises?

Before facts are consulted, a prior question: is either theory a theorem? Both traditions have at times presented themselves as deductions — realism especially, since its rhetorical power lies in the claim of tragic necessity, that anarchy *entails* competition whatever anyone wants. If the entailment held, evidence would be almost beside the point. It does not hold, on either side, and establishing this cleanly is the first real result, because it converts the debate from a logical to an empirical one — which is where an empirical-first adjudication wants it.

### 3.1 The Hidden Premise in Realism

Take Mearsheimer's five assumptions and grant every one. Anarchy: granted. Offensive capability: granted. Uncertainty about intentions: granted. Survival motive: granted. Rationality: granted. What follows? Strictly — nothing yet. From "I cannot be certain of your intentions" no conduct follows until a *decision rule* is supplied for acting under that uncertainty. The realist conclusion — plan against capabilities, assume the possible predator, compete for relative power — is the output of one particular rule: minimax, worst-case reasoning, the maximization of security in the least favorable world. That rule is defensible; it is not entailed. A Bayesian rule is equally consistent with all five premises: form a prior over the other state's type (predator or security-seeker), update on observed behavior — and behavior is informative, because as the costly-signaling literature shows (Fearon 1994; Kydd 2005), some actions are too expensive for predators to fake: unilateral force reductions, submission to intrusive verification, institutional self-binding, the acceptance of domestic audience costs. Glaser (2010) built an entire rationalist theory on this ground and showed that under specifiable conditions — defense dominance, distinguishable postures, moderate priors — the *rational* strategy for a security-seeker under anarchy is cooperation and reassurance, and worst-case reasoning is not prudence but error, because it manufactures the very spiral it insures against (Jervis 1976; Butterfield 1951 called this the tragic element of Hobbesian fear: each side can see its own peaceful intent but not the other's).

The realist has a reply, and it is the tradition's deepest single thought: the asymmetry of stakes. If I wrongly trust a predator, I may die as a political unit — an unrecoverable outcome; if I wrongly distrust a fellow security-seeker, I merely pay the costs of competition — recoverable. Under catastrophic, irreversible downside, the reply goes, worst-case reasoning is not one rule among many but the uniquely rational one; expected-value calculation is for casinos, not survival. This is a serious argument and it will return at the very bottom of this paper, because it is, as Section 7 argues, the true load-bearing wall of realism. But notice what it is: a claim about the correct *loss function* under uncertainty, plus an empirical claim that the downside is in fact catastrophic and unhedgeable. The second claim is testable — how often, in the modern system, does misplaced trust actually kill states? (Section 3.2 answers: since 1945, essentially never.) The first claim is not testable at all; it is a stance toward risk. The deductive engine, in other words, runs only when fueled by a premise realists rarely state: that the rational response to uncertainty about others is to insure against their worst type at any spiral cost. Expose that premise and realism does not collapse — but it ceases to be a theorem and becomes a wager, one whose reasonableness depends on measurable features of the environment.

Two further cracks in the deduction deserve record. First, the canonical texts are less deterministic than the tradition built on them. Hobbes himself, in the very chapter that gives realism its state-of-nature imagery (Leviathan, ch. 13), observes that sovereigns in their armed posture toward one another do not thereby produce the misery that individuals in the state of nature suffer, because states uphold the industry of their subjects — that is, the founding realist text explicitly denies that international anarchy replicates the war of all against all, conceding at the origin the elasticity liberals build on. And Thucydides, realism's oldest authority, is a tragedian, not a cheerleader: the Athenians who tell the Melians that the strong do what they can and the weak suffer what they must are, within a few books, destroyed at Syracuse by the hubris their own doctrine licensed. The ur-text of realism contains realism's refutation-by-narrative: a state that fully internalizes the doctrine over-expands and dies. Second, if survival is the master goal, the theory needs states to be good at pursuing it, yet the historical record realists themselves cite — Napoleon, Wilhelmine and Nazi Germany, Imperial Japan — is a record of great powers catastrophically miscalculating in the direction of *over*-aggression, which defensive realists explain by importing domestic pathologies (Snyder 1991, on cartelized polities selling imperial myths). But every such import concedes Moravcsik's foundation: the box must be opened; structure alone does not fix conduct.

### 3.2 The Selection Defense and Its Empirical Off-Switch

Waltz's answer to all first-image and second-image objections was elegant: the theory does not require that states be rational or realist; it requires only that the system rewards those who behave as if they were and eliminates those who do not. Like firms in a competitive market, states are disciplined by selection and socialization; the logic of anarchy asserts itself through the failures of those who ignore it. This is realism's evolutionary defense, and it is logically sound. It is also, and this is one of the most underweighted facts in the entire debate, running on an environment that no longer exists. Fazal (2007) documented that violent state death — conquest and elimination of the kind that partitioned Poland and consumed the Italian city-states — was a regular feature of international life for centuries and then essentially *ceased after 1945*. Zacher (2001) traced the hardening of the territorial-integrity norm; Altman (2020) showed that even attempted conquest shrank in ambition across the post-war era, from grabs of whole states to grabs of border slivers. Whatever the cause — nuclear weapons, American hegemony, normative change, or all three, a contest Section 4.7 stages — the selection pressure that gives structural realism its enforcement mechanism has been switched off for eighty years. An anarchy that no longer kills the trusting is a different anarchy: a zoo, not a jungle. States can now survive decades of "irrational" institutional faith, generous interdependence, and thin armies — Costa Rica has no army at all and persists — without being selected out. The realist can retort that the switch-off is itself power-dependent and reversible, and 2014 and 2022 are the retort's evidence; that argument is joined properly in 4.7. The point here is narrower and deductive: Waltz's mechanism is not a timeless feature of anarchy but a historical variable, and for the entire lifetime of the modern debate its value has been near zero. A selection argument without selection is a just-so story.

### 3.3 Liberalism's Own Gap

Symmetry requires the same knife on the other throat. Liberalism is no theorem either. From Moravcsik's foundation — individuals first, states as transmission belts — nothing follows about peace until mechanisms are specified, and each specified mechanism is contestable. Consider the democratic-peace claim, liberalism's crown jewel. Why exactly would democracies not fight each other? The literature offers at least four mechanisms, and they matter differently. The *normative* mechanism: democracies externalize their domestic norms of peaceful dispute resolution and trust other democracies to do the same (Owen 1994). The *institutional-constraint* mechanism: leaders who must answer to electorates and legislatures cannot easily start costly wars, and two mutually constrained states cannot get to war (Kant's original wager). The *information* mechanism: democratic transparency and audience costs make democracies' threats and assurances credible, so bargaining fails less often (Fearon 1994). The *selection* mechanism: democracies, fearing electoral punishment, choose only wars they can win, which changes which wars occur rather than how many (Reiter and Stam 2002). These are not interchangeable. The normative mechanism predicts democracies will not even covertly attack one another; the constraint mechanism predicts exactly the opposite — that leaders will route around constraint through secrecy. As Section 4.3 shows, the covert record decides between them, and not in the direction liberal rhetoric prefers. Rosato (2003) pressed the general point: a correlation with four candidate mechanisms, some of which fail on inspection, is not yet a causal law, and a theory that cannot say *why* its regularity holds cannot say *when* it will stop holding — which, for a theory that underwrites democracy-promotion as security policy, is not an academic defect.

The same gap runs through the other legs. Commercial liberalism must explain why interdependence pacifies rather than merely creating hostages and pressure points — the vulnerability that Farrell and Newman (2019) showed can be weaponized by whoever sits at the network's chokepoints. Institutionalism must explain how institutions bind at the moment binding is needed — when a great power's core interests cut against compliance — given that the enforcement problem institutions solve for medium stakes reappears, unsolved, at existential stakes. Liberalism's honest generic form is therefore not "cooperation prevails" but "cooperation prevails *where* its supporting conditions hold," and that "where" is a promissory note the empirical sections must cash.

### 3.4 Interim Verdict

Neither theory deduces its conclusions. Realism's necessity dissolves into a decision rule plus an empirical claim about the lethality of the environment; liberalism's optimism dissolves into a set of mechanisms plus empirical claims about their operating conditions. This is a substantive result, not a draw: it means the debate is *decidable in principle by evidence* to a far greater degree than its metaphysical framing suggests, and it dictates the shape of the tribunal that follows. Each side's load-bearing empirical claims must be isolated and tested; the residue that survives testing on both sides — the loss-function stance, the value commitments — must be quarantined and examined separately, at the bottom, for what it is.

## 4. The Empirical Tribunal

### 4.0 Rules of Evidence

Because this paper's brief demands explicit reflection on when historical examples suffice, the rules are fixed here, before any case is called, so that evidential weight cannot be assigned after seeing which side it favors.

Rule one: a single case can logically falsify only a strict universal claim, and even then only if the auxiliary assumptions connecting theory to observation hold — Duhem's problem never sleeps. World War One refutes "high interdependence makes war impossible" (a universal) but is nearly weightless against "interdependence lowers the probability of war" (a probabilistic claim), because probabilistic claims are tested by distributions, not incidents. Much of the debate's noise consists of hurling single cases at probabilistic claims and probabilities at universal ones.

Rule two: a single case *can* powerfully test a mechanism. Process tracing inside one well-documented episode can establish that a proposed causal pathway did or did not operate there — that decision-makers did or did not reason as the theory requires — and can demonstrate possibility ("this can happen") or impossibility-in-fact ("the mechanism was absent even under ideal conditions"). This is why the July Crisis of 1914, the single most intensively documented decision sequence in history, carries evidential weight far beyond its n of one, and why Section 4.4 spends time inside it.

Rule three: out-of-sample prediction is the gold standard, and it is rare enough to be precious. When a theory's leading proponents commit in print to a forecast before the outcome — as structural realists did about post–Cold War Europe in 1990, as liberal architects of engagement did about China in 2000, as Mearsheimer and Walt did about Iraq in 2002-03 — the result is the closest thing this field gets to an experiment. This paper treats such episodes as first-class evidence and keeps score for both sides, because as it happens, both sides own scalps and both own wounds, and that symmetry is itself a datum.

Rule four: beware the vividness premium and selection on the dependent variable. Canonical cases (1914, Munich, Cuba) do outsized work because they are dramatic and documented, not because they are representative; wars are studied, while the dogs that did not bark — crises resolved, dyads that never fought — are the denominator and must be counted. Statistical work exists precisely to supply denominators, and where statistics and canonical cases conflict, the presumption runs to the denominator, subject to Rule two.

Rule five: when both theories claim the same fact, the fact adjudicates nothing until the theories' *further* commitments are made to diverge and tested. Much of Sections 4.1–4.7 consists of forcing such divergences.

### 4.1 Proposition R1: Institutions Are Epiphenomenal; Alliances Track Threats

The cleanest available test of realism's institutional nihilism was scheduled by history itself. In 1990-93, with the Soviet threat dissolving, structural realism's leading figures made concrete forecasts. Mearsheimer (1990), in "Back to the Future," predicted that with bipolarity gone, NATO's cohesion would decay, American forces would leave Europe, Germany would likely seek nuclear weapons, and multipolar security competition would return to the continent — he suggested we would soon miss the Cold War. Waltz argued in the same period that NATO, having lost its cause, would wither — its years, he said in effect, were numbered — because alliances are functions of threats and cannot outlive them. These were exactly the bold, falsifiable, out-of-sample commitments Rule three prizes, derived honestly from the theory's core: institutions reflect power and threat; remove the threat, and the institutional shell should crumble.

The forecast failed, and failed comprehensively, for a generation. NATO did not dissolve; it expanded three times over, integrated former Warsaw Pact members, developed missions its founders never imagined, and in 2023-24 absorbed Finland and Sweden — its largest functional expansion since 1955 — while the European Union, far from stalling, executed Maastricht, monetary union, and eastward enlargement in the very window the theory marked for decay. Germany did not go nuclear; it went further into institutional self-binding than any great power in history. On Rule three this is a first-class result: structural realism's flagship prediction about the flagship region was wrong, out of sample, for thirty years, and the liberal-institutionalist prediction of persistence and deepening (Keohane, Nye, and the early 1990s institutionalists made it explicitly) was right.

Now the deconstruction, because the realist reply is strong and the exchange must be run to its floor. Call it the *Hegemonic Reduction*: NATO and the EU persisted, realists answer, not because institutions have independent force but because American hegemony continued to pay for order and suppress the security competition that would otherwise have resumed; the institutions are the hegemon's furniture, and Waltz himself, revising in 2000, relocated the prediction — the pathologies would emerge as unipolarity eroded. On this reading the 2010s and 2020s are the vindication on delay: great-power rivalry returned, Europe's demilitarized holiday ended at Crimea, and the institutions revealed themselves unable to prevent the largest European war since 1945. The reply has genuine force and Section 4.7 credits its Ukrainian component in full. But run the counter-deconstruction. First, the Hegemonic Reduction quietly abandons the original theory: "alliances cannot outlive their threats" and "alliances persist as long as a hegemon underwrites them" are different theories with different mechanisms, and sliding from the first to the second after the first failed is precisely the auxiliary-hypothesis shuffle Lakatos diagnosed and Vasquez (1997) formally charged realism with; the APSR forum that followed — Waltz, Christensen and Snyder, Elman and Elman, Schweller, Walt all replying — is the debate's most explicit methodological reckoning, and the fair summary is that even realism's defenders conceded the universalist form was being traded away for scope-restricted descendants. Second, and deeper: if order's existence depends on the hegemon (a power variable) but order's *form* — multilateral, rule-based, self-binding, membership-open — depends on the hegemon's domestic regime, then the reduction has smuggled in the liberal variable. Ikenberry (2001) made the point structurally: victorious hegemons choose among imperial extraction, abandonment, and constitutional self-restraint, and the United States chose institutionalized restraint *because of what kind of state it domestically was*; the counterfactual Soviet-led order — the Warsaw Pact — existed, and its form (coercive, extractive, held by tanks in Budapest and Prague) differed exactly as regime-type theory predicts. The strongest available synthesis of this whole strand, then, is neither side's slogan but a sentence both must sign: *power selects which ideas get built into order; ideas determine what the selected order does.* That sentence ends the deconstruction chain — each side's residual objection to it ("ideas are cheap without power" / "power is blind without ideas") is answered within it.

Verdict: the universal epiphenomenality claim is falsified with high confidence; the threat-tracking law of alliances is falsified in its strict form with high confidence; the hegemony-dependence of the liberal order is a live, partially supported, and — decisively — *currently testable* claim, because American relative decline and domestic turbulence are running the experiment realists specified. If the institutional order substantially holds through a period of genuinely diminished hegemonic underwriting, the reduction fails; if it unravels in step with hegemony, the reduction stands. This is a specified missing fact, not a stalemate — the debate here has been converted into a forecast with a deadline, which is what adjudication under Rule three looks like.

### 4.2 Proposition R2: States Balance Against Power

Balancing is realism's signature regularity: concentrations of power call forth counter-coalitions, as they did against Philip II, Louis XIV, Napoleon, Wilhelm, and Hitler. The post-1991 world staged the test at maximal scale: the largest single-state concentration of relative power in modern history, the United States at unipolarity. The theory's expectation was unambiguous — Waltz wrote that unbalanced power is the great danger and that balancing against it was coming. For over two decades, nothing resembling a hard balance emerged: no anti-American military coalition, no arms build-up race to parity, and the second-tier powers mostly bandwagoned into the hegemon's institutions instead. The auxiliary repairs arrived on schedule: Walt's (1987) balance-of-*threat* amendment (states balance against perceived threat, not raw power, and the U.S. was distant and benign); "soft balancing" (diplomatic friction re-described as balancing); unipolarity as a special structure in which balancing is temporarily infeasible. Each repair has a circularity problem of ascending severity — threat is partly constituted by perception, so balance-of-threat risks predicting balancing wherever balancing occurs and its absence wherever absent; soft balancing is unfalsifiable in a world where diplomatic disagreement always exists. Vasquez's charge of degeneration lands harder here than anywhere else. Schweller (2006) responded from inside realism with the most intellectually honest move available: *underbalancing is real and systematic*, and explaining it requires domestic variables — elite fragmentation, regime vulnerability — which is to say, realism's own repair for its signature prediction is to open the black box liberalism said must be opened. Meanwhile the delayed evidence arrived: from 2014 and decisively after 2022, a Sino-Russian alignment with genuine military-industrial content hardened against the United States, which realists read as balancing resumed, twenty years late but on theory. Perhaps; but note against *what* it consolidated — after NATO expansion and an American turn to explicit containment of China — which fits balance-of-threat (states organize against those who behave threateningly toward them) at least as well as balance-of-power, and balance-of-threat, as just noted, is the version that has traded law-like content for accuracy.

Verdict: balancing as a strict systemic law is falsified with high confidence by the unipolar decades; balancing as a tendency, contingent on threat perception and domestic capacity, is well supported with medium-high confidence — and is no longer distinguishable in kind from a liberal-compatible claim, since "perception" and "domestic capacity" are unit-level variables. On the Lakatos ledger: the universalist belt degenerated; the conditional descendant is progressive but has purchased its accuracy by conceding the opposing program's foundational point. That pattern — repair-by-concession — will recur, on both sides.

### 4.3 Proposition L1: Democracies Do Not Fight One Another

This is liberalism's strongest empirical asset and therefore where the full dialectic must be staged, wave by wave, until it genuinely stops.

The base finding: across the universe of interstate wars since 1815, clear cases of two consolidated democracies waging full interstate war against each other are absent or vanishingly rare, a regularity robust across most codings and the reason for Levy's "empirical law" remark. Statistically, Russett and Oneal (2001) found the dyadic democracy effect large and significant while controlling for alliance, capability ratio, trade, and geography, with all three Kantian legs contributing independently.

First wave of deconstruction — *the definitional gerrymander*. Critics observe that the finding survives partly by coding decisions: the War of 1812 (Britain's franchise), the American Civil War (not interstate), Wilhelmine Germany 1914 (a strong elected Reichstag, but executive war powers held by the Kaiser — excluded), Spain 1898 (democracy discounted), Kargil 1999 (India and Pakistan both electorally governed — usually handled by thresholds on democracy scores or war-death counts). Each exclusion is individually defensible; their conjunction invites Spiro's (1994) suspicion that the category "democracy" has been tuned until the theorem holds. Reply: the codings were established in datasets (Polity) built for other purposes before most tests, which blunts the tuning charge; and tightening definitions in *either* direction — requiring longer consolidation, or loosening to include marginal cases — still leaves the modern core (post-1945 consolidated democracies) with zero wars across thousands of dyad-years. Counter-reply: dyad-years are an inflationary unit — Botswana-and-Iceland-at-peace-in-1987 is not evidence of anything — so the denominator must be restricted to politically relevant dyads with real conflict opportunity; done properly (contiguous or major-power dyads), the democratic zero survives but the statistical miracle shrinks toward something evidence can actually support. This wave ends in a narrowed but standing finding.

Second wave — *the confound of common interest*. Farber and Gowa (1995) showed the effect is statistically significant only after 1945 — precisely when democracies shared a Soviet threat and an American alliance system. Perhaps the Cold War, not democracy, kept the peace among them. Reply: the effect persisted and even strengthened after 1991, when the common threat vanished — the confound's own out-of-sample test, and it failed; moreover intra-alliance peace is itself partly endogenous to regime type (why did the democratic alliance cohere consensually while the socialist one required invasions of its own members?), which routes the confound back through the liberal variable. This wave ends mostly resolved in the finding's favor.

Third wave — *rival causes*. Gartzke (2007) argued the real pacifier is capitalist development and market openness, which correlate with democracy and can absorb its coefficient in some specifications; Gibler (2012) argued causality is reversed — settled borders remove the territorial threats that both militarize states *and* prevent democratization, so stable borders cause democracy and peace, and the dyadic finding is a spurious child of geography. These are the serious ones, because they are not debunkings but identification problems: democracy, development, and settled borders travel together historically, and no randomized assignment of regime type exists. The honest status: the dyadic correlation is robust to most controls, but the causal partition among democracy, markets, and territorial settlement remains genuinely unsettled — a live scientific question, not a talking point for either camp. Note carefully what this does and does not concede to realism: nothing, directly. Gibler's territorial mechanism and Gartzke's commercial mechanism are *liberal-family* rivals to republican liberalism; the intra-family fight leaves realism outside the room, since none of the three contenders operates through power balancing.

Fourth wave — *the mechanism audit*, and here the record cuts in a direction that must be faced squarely because it involves evidence of exactly the kind this paper's brief demands be confronted rather than curated. If the normative mechanism were true — democracies trust and respect one another as a matter of externalized principle — then democracies should not attack one another covertly either. They have. The United States moved covertly against elected governments repeatedly: Iran in 1953, where Operation Ajax deposed Mosaddegh's parliamentary government; Guatemala 1954; Chile's elected government destabilized to the brink of the 1973 coup; and others. Downes and Lilley (2010) formalized the point: the covert record is fatal to the *normative* mechanism in its strong form. But — and this is the dialectically beautiful twist — the same record *supports* the institutional-constraint mechanism, indeed almost perversely confirms it: leaders resorted to covert action precisely because overt war against another democracy was domestically unsellable; secrecy is what evasion of a real constraint looks like. A constraint that leaders must tunnel under is a constraint that exists. So the covert-action evidence does not destroy the democratic peace; it performs surgery on it — amputating the flattering normative story, leaving the colder institutional one — and in doing so sharpens the theory's predictions: expect overt democratic-dyad peace to hold, expect covert conflict wherever executives can act unobserved, and expect the peace to weaken wherever executive war powers escape legislative and electoral constraint, including *within* formally democratic states undergoing executive aggrandizement. That last derived prediction is uncomfortably testable in the present decade.

Fifth wave — *the boundary evidence*. Mansfield and Snyder (2005) showed that states in transition to democracy, with mass politics mobilized before institutions consolidate, are *more* war-prone than stable autocracies — nationalism outruns constraint. Both camps claim this: liberals as a refinement (consolidation, not elections, is the variable), realists as proof that the pacifying agent was never regime type but the settled great-power context in which consolidation happens. And the monadic claim — that democracies are generally more peaceful toward everyone — finds little support: democracies fight non-democracies enthusiastically, including wars of choice and empire. The peace is dyadic, or it is nothing.

End of the chain — no further deconstructive move known to the literature succeeds against the narrowed core, and the narrowed core is this: among consolidated democracies, overt interstate war is absent across the modern record at a rate no plausible confound has yet fully absorbed (high confidence in the regularity); the mechanism is institutional-informational rather than normative (medium-high confidence); the causal share of democracy against development and territorial settlement is unresolved (genuinely open — specified missing fact: identification strategies, e.g., discontinuities around democratization episodes with settled borders held fixed); and extrapolations from the dyadic law to democratization-as-pacification are unsupported and in transition cases reversed. Realism's residual position here is reduced to the Farber-Gowa-style external account, which failed its post-1991 out-of-sample test. On this proposition, and it should be said plainly because this paper will say the reverse elsewhere: liberalism holds the ground.

### 4.4 Proposition L2: Interdependence Pacifies

Now the mirror image — the liberal claim that has taken the worst beating, and where the reflection this paper's brief demands (what exactly did the great counterexample falsify?) must be performed inside the case.

The naïve claim, circa 1910: economic interdependence among the great powers had made major war irrational and therefore unlikely-to-impossible. Anglo-German trade stood at historic highs; the City of London financed the world; Norman Angell's *The Great Illusion* argued conquest could no longer pay. Four years later the interdependent core of the world economy fed itself into Flanders. If ever a single case were entitled to kill a theory under Rule one, this is it: the claim was near-universal, the conditions were maximal, the outcome was the negation. And yet precision about *what died* is everything. Angell's actual thesis — that conquest would not profit the conqueror — was not refuted but grimly vindicated by 1918: the victors were ruined too. What died was the *inference* from "war does not pay" to "war will not happen," an inference that requires states to be unitary profit-calculators, which the July Crisis, examined under Rule two, shows they were not. Inside the documented six weeks: a security-dilemma spiral in its purest recorded form (mobilization timetables in which each defensive step was the next state's offensive alarm — the Russian general mobilization forcing the Schlieffen clock); *and* domestic pathologies exactly where Snyder's cartelized-politics model looks for them (a German leadership gambling abroad partly against Social Democracy at home); *and*, per the fifty-year Fischer controversy, genuine contestation over whether Berlin willed the war (Fischer 1961, from the archives) or all sleepwalked into it through interaction effects (Clark 2012). Note the epistemological moral for Rule two: the best-documented case in history still supports two rival readings after a century of archival combat — *but not any reading*; the archives killed the pure-accident story and the pure-innocence story, narrowing the interval without reaching a point. Interval-narrowing, not point-identification, is what historical adjudication achieves at its best, and that standard — not laboratory closure — is the right one to hold this whole debate to.

So the naïve commercial peace is dead; what replaced it? Copeland's trade-expectations theory (1996; 2014): interdependence pacifies when states expect future trade to continue and turns malignant when a dependent state expects cutoff, because dependence-without-expectation is vulnerability, and vulnerability invites preventive grabbing. This retrofits 1914 (German fears of encirclement and future strangulation), genuinely fits the 1930s (Japan's resource panic after Smoot-Hawley and embargo — the clearest case of collapsing trade expectations driving expansion), and — decisive for its scientific standing — issues a live forward prediction: if U.S.-China decoupling deepens and Beijing's expectations of future access collapse, conflict risk over Taiwan rises through the trade-expectations channel specifically. That is falsifiable content, on the clock, and it should be scored when history grades it. Meanwhile the 2022 evidence restructured the whole leg: Europe's gas interdependence with Russia did not prevent the invasion of Ukraine — it was mobilized *by* Russia as a coercive instrument, exactly the chokepoint weaponization Farrell and Newman (2019) had theorized, and then, in the medium run, the dependence was unwound at cost, suggesting asymmetric interdependence is better modeled as a power resource than as a pacifier. The net standing of the leg: monotonic pacification, falsified (high confidence); conditional pacification via expectations, alive with genuine predictive content (medium confidence, pending its Taiwan test); asymmetric interdependence as coercion-enabling, well supported (high confidence). Realism wins the headline here and deserves it — with the footnote that the version of interdependence theory that survives is not a chastened retreat but a sharper instrument than the one that died, which is what progressive problem-shifts look like when they happen honestly.

### 4.5 Proposition L3: Institutions Cause Cooperation Beyond Power

The Keohane-Mearsheimer exchange of 1994-95 fixed the question: do institutions exert independent causal force, or do they merely register the power and interest that would produce the same outcomes anyway? Three decades of evidence sort into a pattern too consistent to ignore. Off the security core, institutions demonstrably bind: the trade regime processed hundreds of disputes with compliance in the large majority of adjudicated cases, including repeated instances of the United States and the European Union losing to small complainants and complying; technical regimes (aviation, postal, health reporting, standards) run the daily world with a reliability no one bothers to theorize because it never fails interestingly. At the security core, institutions bend or break when a great power's central interests engage: China took Philippines v. China (2016) to a comprehensive legal defeat under UNCLOS and ignored it without measurable cost; Russia exited or violated the treaty architecture of European security seriatim; the United States, when the WTO's Appellate Body ruled against its practices too often, strangled the body itself by blocking appointments. The Non-Proliferation Treaty splits the difference instructively: dozens of technically capable states forwent nuclear weapons within its framework — a genuine institutional-normative achievement realism did not predict — while the states that most needed weapons for security (Israel, India, Pakistan, North Korea) simply took them, treaty or no.

Each camp will claim this pattern whole; Rule five forbids that. Force the divergence. If institutions were pure epiphenomena, their *design* should be decorative — yet Koremenos, Lipson and Snidal (2001) showed design features vary systematically and predictably with the underlying cooperation problem (escape clauses where uncertainty is high, intrusive monitoring where defection pays, restricted membership where externalities concentrate), which is engineering, not decoration; states behave as if institutional machinery has effects worth paying bargaining costs to shape. Conversely, if institutions could bind against core power, the Appellate Body would have survived American displeasure and the 2016 award would govern the South China Sea; they could not and it does not. The adjudicated result is therefore a *scope law*, and it is one of this tribunal's firmest products: institutional force is real and causal in the vast middle of international life, and it decays as issues approach great-power existential stakes, approaching zero at the core — because the enforcement problem institutions solve at medium stakes reappears unsolved at stakes for which no reputational or linkage price compensates. High confidence. Notice, finally, the dialectical structure hiding in the standard liberal excuse and the standard realist sneer, because it is the single most elegant knot in the entire debate: when liberals explain the League's collapse or the UN's paralysis by saying states refused to give the institution real power, they restate realism's premise — sovereign self-help — in the vocabulary of apology; and when realists explain eighty years of institutionalized peace as hegemony's artifact, they concede that anarchy's output depends on who orders it and how, which is the liberal variable in a realist uniform. Each side's standard move at the limit *is* the other side's thesis. That is not a stalemate; it is the strongest possible evidence that the two theories are describing one system's two regimes, a conclusion Section 7 will formalize.

### 4.6 Proposition R3: Socialization Runs Toward Power Politics, Not Away From It

Realism predicts convergence: whatever a state's domestic character, the system teaches realpolitik. Liberalism predicts the reverse potential: engagement, interdependence, and institutional enmeshment can transform preferences. The largest deliberate test ever run was the China engagement bet — two decades of American and European policy explicitly premised (in official argument, from the WTO-accession debates onward) on the proposition that trade and enmeshment would liberalize China politically and socialize it into stakeholder conduct. The bet's political leg failed out of sample and should be scored as liberalism's largest modern predictive wound, symmetrical to realism's 1990 Europe wound: China grew rich and became *more* autocratic and more assertive, and the tighter the enmeshment grew, the more instruments of leverage it acquired. Russia ran the smaller parallel experiment — G8 membership, energy integration, resets — to the same terminus. The realist reading writes itself: preferences did not transform; power grew and behavior followed power.

Run the counter-deconstruction anyway, because the ledger is not one-sided. First, the same decades contain the strongest preference-transformation cases in recorded history: France and Germany, three wars in seventy years, transformed into a security community in which war is not merely unlikely but *unthought* — planning staffs do not game it — under identical anarchy and identical geography; the only variables that moved were regime type, institutional enmeshment, and generational ideational change. Structural realism cannot explain same-structure-different-behavior of this magnitude and mostly does not try, invoking the American pacifier — which, per 4.1, imports the hegemon's regime type through the side door. Second, the engagement bet's failure has a scope reading: transformation occurred where enmeshment reached deep into domestic institutions (accession conditionality for EU candidates rewired whole legal orders) and failed where a hard authoritarian state controlled the interface and harvested the gains while firewalling the politics — which converts the finding from "socialization is false" into "socialization requires domestic transmission channels autocracies can sever," a claim with content and, again, forward predictions. Third, honesty requires recording what cannot be recovered: the strong liberal expectation of the 1990s — history's direction, convergence on market democracy — is gone as a scientific claim, and the camp that held it has largely stopped defending it. Verdict: transformation is real, slow, reversible, and channel-dependent (medium-high confidence); its strong automatic version is falsified out of sample (high confidence); realist convergence-on-realpolitik is falsified by the European security community with equal confidence. Both universal socialization stories are dead; the conditional ones pass in opposite regions of the same parameter space.

### 4.7 Proposition L4: War and Conquest Are Declining Phenomena

The macro-claim, and the place where the two live crises of the 2020s must be faced. The pattern first: no direct great-power war since 1945 — the Long Peace (Gaddis 1986) — a stretch without precedent since the Concert era; violent state death effectively zero since 1945 (Fazal 2007); territorial conquest attempts collapsed in frequency and shrank in ambition (Zacher 2001; Altman 2020); battle deaths per capita trending downward across decades (Pinker 2011). Candidate causes, each with a champion: nuclear deterrence making great-power victory meaningless (Jervis 1989 — a realist-compatible cause, note, since it operates through fear); bipolar then unipolar stability (Waltz; Gilpin); the American security umbrella suppressing regional spirals; economic transformation making land valueless relative to trade; and ideational change — Mueller's (1989) thesis that major war is undergoing the trajectory of dueling and slavery, sliding from "costly option" to "not an option," an exit from the choice set itself. The causes are confounded across the same seventy years and cannot be cleanly partitioned; that is a permanent limitation, honestly recorded.

But before any causal story, a statistical brake that this paper is obligated by its own rules to apply against the side the brake damages — which is, here, the liberal-progressive side. Braumoeller (2019) and Clauset (2018) showed that interstate war sizes follow a fat-tailed distribution in which seventy or eighty calm years are entirely compatible with an unchanged underlying process; given the historical variance, distinguishing "the process changed" from "we drew lucky" requires on the order of another century of data. The single most important dependent variable in the entire debate — the propensity for great-power war — is therefore *statistically starving*: the facts that would decide it have not finished occurring. This is not a rhetorical humility; it is a computable one, and it means every confident claim that war is obsolete, and every confident claim that the Long Peace is an illusion, outruns the evidence symmetrically.

What the tails cannot settle, the conquest data partially can, because conquest frequency is not fat-tailed in the same way — it is a count of attempts, and the count collapsed. Here the 2014 and 2022 evidence enters, and it must be processed under Rule one with care, because it is the most rhetorically abused evidence in the contemporary debate. Russia's seizure of Crimea and full invasion of Ukraine are, on the liberal-normative account, norm violations — and single violations do not refute norms as statistical regularities (murder rates do not refute the criminal law). But that easy reply undersells what happened: the violator is a permanent Security Council member attempting the first outright conquest-and-annexation war by a great power in Europe since 1945, and norms among great powers are few enough that one defection is not statistical noise; it is a probe of whether the norm was ever load-bearing or merely epiphenomenal to the distribution of power (the realist reading: the taboo held while American preponderance enforced it and eroded as preponderance did). The counter-evidence to the realist reading, however, is carried by the same case: the invasion's *reception*. A norm that was mere parchment would collapse quietly; instead the violation triggered coordinated cost-imposition by dozens of states at real economic self-harm, the largest sanctions cascade ever assembled, accelerated alliance enlargement, and — striking for the deductive layer — the near-universal refusal, including by Russia's partners, to *recognize* the annexations, recognition being the currency in which the territorial-integrity norm is banked. Compare 1772: Poland's partition was ratified by the society of states within a few years. The 2022 probe thus returned a split result with unusual clarity: the norm no longer physically prevents great-power conquest attempts (realist point, scored), and the norm still organizes the collective response and withholds the legitimacy that would convert seizure into settled possession (liberal-constructivist point, scored). Which force wins the decade is a specified open fact.

And the causal fight over the invasion itself — realism's most publicized contemporary claim — must be adjudicated rather than waved at. Mearsheimer's argument (2014, and after): NATO's open-door march toward Ukraine, declared at Bucharest 2008, predictably triggered a great power's sphere-defense; the West caused the crisis in the sense of running a foreseeable spiral, exactly as realist logic warned, and the warning is on record in advance — a Rule-three credit that must be paid. The identification problems, however, are severe, and they are not partisan quibbles but structural. First discriminating test: if NATO proximity per se were the operative threat, Finland's 2023 accession — doubling NATO's land border with Russia, at the approaches to Murmansk's second-strike bastion — should have produced a security crisis; it produced rhetoric and force redeployments *away* from that border. The realist rejoinder — Ukraine is categorically different: Black Sea geography, historical core, size — is available but expensive, because "historical core" is an identity variable, and a structural theory that needs identity variables to save its flagship case has paid the Moravcsik toll at the door. Second test: timing and stated reasons — the maximal invasion came in 2022, not at the 2008 declaration or after the 2014 seizure, following years in which Ukraine's NATO accession had become *less* proximate, while the Russian president's own extended 2021 essay grounded the coming war in historical-national unity claims, and the war's opening move was a decapitation strike on Kyiv's government, an objective (regime replacement) that neutrality-seeking sphere-defense does not require but imperial restoration does. Third: the McFaul-school regime-security reading — that a consolidating Ukrainian democracy next door was intolerable to Russian autocracy as an *example*, independent of armies — predicts exactly the observed target set (the political order, the identity, the elections) better than the missile-geography reading does. None of this refutes the spiral component; multi-causation is the honest verdict, and the July-1914 lesson from 4.4 applies — interval-narrowing: the archives of this war, when they open, will kill some of these readings, and the theories have been forced, usefully, to commit in advance. What can be scored now: realism's *prescriptive* warning (expansion without the intent to defend the expanded is the worst of all worlds) is substantially vindicated; realism's *descriptive* monocause (security spiral, full stop) is not, and rests on auxiliary identity-imports that concede the unit-level point. The register distinction is doing decisive work, as Section 5.4 will generalize.

For completeness the tribunal records realism's other clean Rule-three credit: in 2002-03, the field's leading realists publicly opposed the invasion of Iraq, predicting quagmire and regional destabilization against the confident expectations of liberal and neoconservative hawks; they were right, out of sample, in print. The credit carries an asterisk that must be attached with equal prominence: Iraq was a war realist *theory* says should not have happened — a secure hegemon electing a crusading war of choice — so the episode vindicates realism as prescription (prudence, the sharp eye for hubris) while embarrassing realism as description (the system's most-watched state ignored structural incentives, at scale, driven by ideology and domestic politics: unit-level all the way down). Realism keeps being right as a warning in exact proportion as states keep falsifying it as a law.

Read the columns, not the rows. Every universal claim, on both sides, is dead — each killed by well-established particulars, several by out-of-sample failures against the other side's out-of-sample successes. Every conditional descendant is alive, and — the finding that organizes everything downstream — the conditions that revive each theory are the *other theory's variables*: realism survives by importing perception, domestic capacity, and regime type; liberalism survives by importing enforcement limits, hegemonic underwriting, and existential-stakes decay. The tribunal's output is therefore not a winner but a shape: two mechanism-families, each empirically real, each dominant in a specifiable region of a shared parameter space, each degenerate exactly when it claims the whole space. What prevents this from being toothless eclecticism — the obvious and correct objection — is that the regions are specifiable and the boundary generates forecasts, several now on the clock (hegemonic-withdrawal test, Taiwan trade-expectations test, conquest-norm consolidation test, executive-aggrandizement test on the democratic peace). Section 7 gives the parameter space its structure. But first, the question the whole tribunal begs: if this much *was* decidable, why did eighty years of professionals not settle it? The answer is that four structural features of the domain cap what tribunals like this one can deliver — and mapping that ceiling precisely is the descent's next layer.

## 5. Why the Tribunal Cannot Close: Four Structural Obstacles

### 5.1 Small N and Fat Tails

The debate's most important dependent variables are rare events. Great-power wars arrive a handful per century; hegemonic transitions, once or twice; and the size distribution of wars is fat-tailed, so the moments that matter most are precisely the ones for which frequencies are least informative. Section 4.7's computable humility generalizes: for the central question — has the probability of catastrophic war changed? — the world has not yet generated enough data to answer, and no methodological ingenuity substitutes for the missing draws. This is a permanent epistemic discount that international-relations theory carries relative to the observational sciences it otherwise resembles (epidemiology, climatology, macroeconomics): same inferential logic, smaller N on the tail events, and the discount should be stated as such rather than laundered into either camp's confidence.

### 5.2 The Counterfactual Circle

Every historical judgment in Section 4 leaned on a counterfactual: NATO absent, would Europe have re-spiraled? Democracy absent, would the democratic dyads have fought? Expansion absent, would Ukraine stand unattacked? Counterfactuals are not observed; they are generated by theories — and the theories are the very things on trial. The structure is the Münchhausen trilemma wearing archival gloves: justify a case-reading and you regress to a theory, which regresses to cases, which regress to readings — circle; or you stop at a "settled" reading — dogmatism; or you never stop — regress. The trilemma is escaped here exactly as it is escaped in the other observational sciences, through three partial exits, each used in Section 4 and each honestly limited. Out-of-sample prediction (the gold standard; but rare, and both camps hold scalps — 4.1 against realism, 4.6 against liberalism, 4.7 partially for realism — so it disciplines without crowning). Micro-foundational evidence from outside the case universe: laboratory public-goods experiments (Ostrom 1990; Fehr and Gächter 2000) establish that humans are neither Hobbesian egoists nor Kantian cosmopolitans but *conditional cooperators* whose cooperation collapses without monitoring and punishment and stabilizes with them — a result that, transposed, underwrites both realism's suspicion of unenforced promises and liberalism's claim that enforcement technology transforms outcomes, and thereby dissolves the anthropological version of the debate at the micro level while relocating the real question to whether anarchy can supply the enforcement functions domestic orders supply (Section 4.5's scope law answers: partially, decaying with stakes). And process tracing, which narrows intervals inside cases without closing them (4.4's standard). Three exits, all partial: the circle is not broken but widened enough to do science in — which is precisely the epistemic situation of every historical science, and the point at which further skepticism stops being about international relations and starts being about geology.

### 5.3 Reflexivity

The deepest structural obstacle, and the one that most changes what "adjudication" can mean. The theories under trial are not external descriptions of planetary motion; they are beliefs held by the actors whose behavior they describe, and the beliefs feed back. A state elite convinced of realism arms, distrusts, and preempts — and thereby confirms realism in its neighbors' experience; the July Crisis is the type case of a fear-spiral in which each government's worst-case reasoning manufactured the world that justified it. A community of states convinced that integration is possible binds, verifies, and reciprocates — and thereby confirms liberalism in its members' experience; postwar Western Europe is the type case of a trust-spiral, trust extended, honored, and compounded until war exited the choice set. Wendt's (1992) constitutive point, stripped of its metaphysics, is this feedback loop: anarchy's "logic" is partly an equilibrium of expectations, and expectations are inputs the theorists themselves supply. Merton named the genus self-fulfilling prophecy. The consequence for adjudication is severe and precise: a theory that alters its own truth conditions when believed cannot be tested against a belief-independent fact of the matter, because in the relevant region there is none — there are multiple equilibria, and theory-choice is partly *path*-choice. This is not mysticism; it is a fact about the domain, and it is the deepest structural reason the debate cannot fully resolve empirically at the global level: part of what the theories describe is downstream of which theory the describers act on. It also, and this should be stated as the finding it is, converts a tract of the "descriptive" debate into a *policy* question in disguise — which spiral to feed — and thereby hands the residue to Section 6's decision theory rather than to further data collection. Two boundaries on the point, to keep it honest: reflexivity is not omnipotence — believing interdependence pacifies did not save 1914, and material constraints (geography, nuclear physics, economic scale) bound which equilibria are reachable; and reflexivity cuts both ways as evidence, since the European trust-spiral and the 1914 fear-spiral jointly demonstrate that *both* attractors exist, which is itself the two-regime conclusion again, arrived at by a third independent route.

### 5.4 Register-Switching

The debate's perpetual-motion machine. Realism oscillates between description (states do pursue power; institutions are epiphenomenal) and prescription (states *should* be prudent, resist crusades, respect spheres) — and the two registers rescue each other in alternation: when states behave un-realistically (Iraq 2003, Wilhelmine risk-lust), the descriptive failure is rebooked as prescriptive vindication ("they ignored realism and paid"); when realist prescription looks amoral, the description is invoked ("we merely report the tragic world"). Liberalism runs the mirrored oscillation: descriptive claims about democratic and commercial peace fund prescriptive crusades (democracy promotion, engagement) whose failures are then rebooked as descriptive refinements ("consolidation was incomplete; the dose was wrong"). Carr (1939) saw the machine at the founding and said the mature thing: pure realism is sterile — it can explain everything and propose nothing, since even "the national interest" is a value smuggled in — and pure utopianism is blind, unable to see power; statecraft and theory alike live in the tension, not at either pole. The methodological rule that stops the machine is simple and this paper has tried to enforce it throughout: every claim carries its register tag, and no failure in one register may be paid for with credit from the other. Applied ruthlessly, the rule reveals how much of the eighty-year war was fought between a description and a prescription that were never in contact — and how much smaller, and more decidable, the genuine disagreement is once the registers are held apart. What remains after the holding-apart is the subject of the final descent.

## 6. The Formal Floor: Bargaining, Commitment, and the Decision-Theoretic Core

If the debate has a load-bearing formal skeleton beneath the schools, Fearon (1995) supplied it, and reaching it is the closest thing to the "deeper analytical level" at which the surface quarrel finally becomes tractable. Begin from the ex-post inefficiency puzzle: war destroys value, so for any war there exists in principle a negotiated settlement both sides prefer to fighting; rationalist war therefore requires an explanation of why bargaining *fails*. Fearon isolated three sufficient frictions. Private information with incentives to misrepresent: states know their own resolve and strength, profit from bluffing, and so cannot credibly communicate, producing wars of erroneous optimism. Commitment problems: even with full information, a state whose relative power is rising cannot credibly promise not to exploit tomorrow's strength, so the declining state may rationally prefer war now to extortion later — the preventive-war logic that runs from Thucydides's Spartans through 1914's "window" talk to present-day transition anxieties (Allison's 2017 case series popularized the pattern; its case codings are contested, but the mechanism is not). Issue indivisibility, mostly reducible in practice to the first two.

The translation is exact, and it reorganizes the entire debate. Realism, in this language, is the claim that the frictions are severe and irreducible where it matters: information about intentions is opaque at the security core and incentives to misrepresent are maximal; commitment problems are insoluble under anarchy because no third party enforces intertemporal promises — anarchy just *is* a commitment-problem generator; therefore bargaining failure, and its anticipation, and the fear of its anticipation, structurally recur. Liberalism is the claim that the frictions are variables with technologies: information friction is reducible by transparency, signaling, and the domestic observability of democracies (Fearon 1994; Kydd 2005 formalized how a rational security-seeker can, through graduated costly concessions, separate itself from a predator in the eyes of a Bayesian observer — trust-building as identification strategy, mathematically respectable); commitment friction is reducible by institutional self-binding, hostage-exchange in the form of interdependence, and constitutional lock-in (Ikenberry's account of 1945 is exactly a commitment-technology story: the hegemon bought cheap acquiescence by making its own power contractually predictable). On the evidence assembled in Section 4, both translations are right in their regions: information technologies demonstrably work (the democratic peace's surviving mechanism is informational-institutional; arms-control verification regimes functioned for decades), and commitment technologies demonstrably decay with stakes (4.5's scope law), with one brute exception — secure second-strike nuclear forces, which solve the commitment problem at the existential level not by promise but by physics: the ability to exploit total victory is removed, so the promise not to seek it becomes credible by default. The Long Peace, in this light, is over-determined by the debate's *joint* machinery: deterrence hardened the commitment floor (realist-compatible), on top of which information and institution technologies could compound cooperation upward (liberal), which is why partisans of each cause find their evidence everywhere — they are looking at one structure's two stories.

What, then, is genuinely left undecided at the formal floor? Strip everything settled and two residues remain, and they should be stated with the precision the descent has earned. First residue: *the prior*. Bayesian trust-building works given some non-degenerate prior that the other is a security-seeker; worst-case reasoning is the limit of a prior collapsed onto predation. Evidence disciplines priors at the margin — track records, regime observability, costly signals — but for the cases that matter (a rising, opaque, grievance-articulating great power), the record is thin exactly where the stakes are thick, and reasonable Bayesians end far apart with no further fact presently available to force convergence. Second residue: *the loss function*. Even fixed priors leave the choice of what to minimize: expected costs (which counsels reciprocation and risk-tolerant reassurance, since spirals are the modal disaster) or worst-case catastrophic loss (which counsels insurance and hedging, since betrayal is the fatal one) — and under the fat tails of 5.1, where expected values are dominated by unpriceable extremes, decision theory itself goes formally quiet: this is Knightian territory, where the axioms that make expected-utility reasoning uniquely rational lose their grip. Add 5.3's reflexivity — the chosen posture feeds the neighbor's evidence and thus partly manufactures the world it bet on — and the residue's final form is visible: a wager, made under irreducible uncertainty about other minds at the scale of states, with catastrophic asymmetric stakes, inside a feedback loop. Every empirical instrument this paper trusts has been applied; they carried the adjudication this far and no further. What kind of commitment is a wager of that shape? That question is no longer social-scientific, and the last section of the descent must change disciplines to answer it.

## 7. The Last Stratum: Hinges, Values, and the Riverbed of Statecraft

### 7.1 What Remains, Stated Exactly

Three commitments survived every empirical and formal instrument. First, the *risk posture*: whether, facing an opaque potential adversary under fat-tailed stakes, one reasons from expected value or from the worst case — Section 6's loss-function residue. Second, the *value order*: whether the survival and autonomy of the state is lexically prior to other goods (the raison d'état that realism prescribes and, despite its self-image as value-free description, cannot do without — Carr's point that a realism with no ought cannot even say why the state should be defended), or whether individual persons are the ultimate units of moral concern and the state their instrument (liberalism's normative individualism, Kant's inheritance). Third, the *regulative stance toward history*: whether directed improvement of the system is a live possibility to be acted on (Kant's guarantee, secularized into liberal practice) or a recurring illusion the tragic structure punishes (realism's cyclical time). None of these is a factual proposition awaiting a decisive observation. The first is a decision rule; the second is an ought, and the is/ought gap that blocks deriving it from any array of facts is not a defect of this debate but a general condition; the third is a stance toward an underdetermined future. Facts constrain all three — the tribunal narrowed the space of tenable postures dramatically, and a risk posture that ignores the conquest-collapse data or a progress-stance that ignores the tails is now simply *uninformed* — but within the narrowed space, facts have gone silent.

### 7.2 The Hinge Structure

Commitments of this kind have a recognizable epistemological form. Wittgenstein's late analysis (1969) described propositions that function not as conclusions within a practice but as the scaffolding that makes the practice's doubts and proofs possible — hinges on which inquiry turns, held fast not by evidence but by everything around them; and his figure for their status was the riverbed: the bank of hard rock and the sand the river shifts are both bed to the water, yet the bed itself is not immutable — it is where movement is slowest, not absent. The two traditions of statecraft exhibit exactly this structure. Inside the realist practice, "in the last resort no one will save us" is not a hypothesis under test; it is the certainty that organizes what counts as evidence, prudence, and negligence — a professional formation, transmitted through ministries and staff colleges, that makes certain doubts (perhaps this adversary's assurances suffice) *unserious* rather than merely improbable. Inside the liberal practice, "the frictions are workable; binding is possible; progress can be banked" plays the same constitutive role, making other doubts (perhaps all of this is parchment) unserious in the same way. This diagnosis explains the debate's phenomenology better than any account yet given: why intelligent professionals process identical events into opposite confirmations (the hinge sorts the evidence before argument begins), why refutation at the surface never propagates downward (surface propositions can fall while the hinge holds the practice together), and why the quarrel feels, from inside each camp, like arguing with someone who "doesn't understand how the world works" — the sensation of hinge-collision, not proposition-collision, precisely what argument between frameworks rather than within one feels like.

Two disciplines keep this diagnosis from dissolving into relativism, and both matter. First, the fork sits *above* a shared riverbed. Both practices stand on the hinges that make observational science possible — induction, the reliability of records, the existence and rough intelligibility of other minds and other states — which is why Sections 3 through 6 could adjudicate as much as they did: the camps share enough bed to run a common tribunal, and the tribunal's verdicts (the dead universals, the scope laws, the two-regime structure) bind both. The disagreement was never global skepticism; it is a fork three layers up, and mapping its exact altitude — everything below it decidable, the fork itself not — is this paper's central product. Second, and against the resigned reading of hinge talk: *hinges are historical*. The riverbed moves; Wittgenstein's own figure says so. And international practice offers something remarkable that the epistemology alone would not predict — hinge formation observable in the historical record. Mueller's (1989) account of major war's obsolescence is, read carefully, a description of a proposition migrating out of the space of live options: dueling and chattel slavery did not become refuted, they became *unthinkable* — exiting the choice set of serious persons, which is the practical form of becoming a hinge. The Franco-German case of 4.6 is the process caught midway in a single dyad: war moved from policy option to bad option to unplanned-for impossibility within two generations. The territorial-integrity norm of 4.7 is a system-wide hinge laid down after 1945 — conquest passing from ordinary statecraft to near-unanimous unthinkability — whose 2014-2022 stress test returned the split verdict recorded there: physically violable, normatively unratifiable. The riverbed shifted once within living memory, in the liberal direction, at continental scale; it is being probed now; and whether the probe becomes erosion is being decided by the collective practice of states in real time. Which yields the paper's final epistemological finding, and the partial restoration of its empirical-first commitment at the very stratum where facts were supposed to have gone silent: the *content* of the rival hinges cannot be adjudicated by evidence, but the *career* of hinges can be observed — we cannot deduce which certainty is correct, but we can watch which certainties the world's practice is currently laying down or washing out. The riverbed itself is under observation.

### 7.3 Closing the Regress

The stopping rule declared in Section 1 can now be executed rather than merely promised. The skeptical regress — every adjudication rests on readings, readings on theories, theories on cases — was halted by three partial exits (5.2) whose joint reliability is exactly the reliability of historical-observational science in general; to doubt further is to doubt geology and epidemiology, a price already declined at the outset. The Münchhausen trilemma is not solved — it never is — but its dogmatic horn is grasped in the only respectable way available: openly, at a marked location, with the mark placed where the sciences already accepted as reliable place theirs, minus an explicitly quantified discount for smaller N (5.1) and reflexivity (5.3). Below the mark lie the three surviving commitments of 7.1, identified as hinges, with their altitudes surveyed and their historical motion logged. The descent has no further floor to find: pressed below this, every remaining question either collapses into general philosophy of science (shared bed, out of scope by the paper's own charter) or into first ethics (the is/ought residue, which no accumulation of international facts crosses). This is what "deconstructed until it is not possible to deconstruct" turns out to mean in practice — not a victor standing over a refuted opponent, but a completed survey: every position's load-bearing wall located, every wall tested, the ones that fell recorded, and the ones that stand identified as standing on commitment rather than proof, on both sides, symmetrically.

## 8. Verdict and the Wager

The adjudication, assembled. As universal covering-law theories, realism and liberalism are both dead, and honest members of each camp have already stopped defending the corpses: realism's laws of balancing, alliance decay, and institutional impotence fell to out-of-sample facts, as did liberalism's laws of commercial peace, socialization-by-engagement, and history's direction. As conditional mechanism-bundles, both are alive, real, and — the tribunal's organizing discovery — *complementary in structure*: each theory's rescue conditions are the other's variables, each side's limit-excuse is the other's thesis, and the two-regime shape recurs through every independent route taken (the empirical scorecard, the micro-foundations, the reflexivity attractors, the bargaining frictions). The best-supported single picture of international politics now available is therefore not a school but a parameter space: realist dynamics are the limit case as information, iteration, enforcement, and second-strike stability go to zero and stakes go existential; liberal dynamics are the limit case in the opposite corner; the observed world since 1945 has sat, unevenly and revocably, well inside the liberal region — its equilibrium genuinely characterized by liberal mechanisms — while realist mechanisms characterize the region's *boundary*: what happens at the security core, off the equilibrium path, and if the supporting parameters (hegemonic underwriting, nuclear stability, the conquest hinge) degrade. Liberal orders, on this reading, do not refute the security dilemma; they parameterize it downward — engineered mitigations of realist gradients. Realism describes the slope; liberalism, the dams; a theory of the river needs both, and mistakes either for the whole at the cost of exactly the predictive failures each side's ledger records.

The picture is falsifiable, and to preempt the eclecticism charge its falsifiers are posted: a wave of great-power conquest *without* prior hegemonic and deterrent breakdown would break the parameterization's realist boundary-assignment; a full interstate war between consolidated democracies would break its liberal core; durable deep cooperation between mutually fearful nuclear rivals *absent* any information or commitment technology would break the friction framework itself; and the live named tests — the hegemonic-withdrawal test of 4.1, the Taiwan trade-expectations test of 4.4, the conquest-ratification test of 4.7, the executive-aggrandizement test of 4.3 — are running now, with the framework's predictions on record.

What the facts finally cannot decide has been cornered into three hinge commitments, and the practical question of the coming decades — the posture to take inside the parameter region where the confidence intervals of both mechanism-families overlap, which is to say, toward a rising, opaque, grievance-bearing great power under fat-tailed stakes — is a wager whose terms this descent has at least made explicit: expected spiral costs against worst-case betrayal costs, under priors that evidence can inform but not fix, inside a feedback loop where the posture chosen becomes part of its own evidence. That the wager exists is not a failure of the analysis; it is the analysis's terminal finding, and knowing precisely where proof ends and commitment begins is the entire difference between a wager made in the dark and one made on the riverbed, in daylight, with the water's movement measured. The debate between realism and liberalism does not resolve, at the bottom, because at the bottom it was never a debate about facts; every factual question it contained has either been answered, or assigned its missing observation, or shown to be starving on a computable schedule. What remains is a choice about which risks a political community will run and what it holds itself to be for — and the only intellectually honest report is that eighty years of evidence have narrowed that choice enormously, and cannot make it.

## 9. Limitations

Four should be recorded without ceremony. The case base is Euro-Atlantic-heavy, as the field's is; the theories were minted from Westphalian experience, and their export to regional systems with different sovereignty practices (pre-colonial South and East Asian orders, contemporary Gulf and African security complexes) is assumed more than shown — the postcolonial critique that both schools universalize one province's history is only partially met by the post-1945 global scope of the conquest and state-death data. Constructivism has been used instrumentally (reflexivity, hinge formation) rather than tried as a third full party, and hierarchy theories (Lake) and the English School — Bull's anarchical *society*, which anticipated the two-regime conclusion by intuition decades before this tribunal reached it by elimination — deserve fuller seats. The quantitative claims summarized here inherit their literatures' specification debates, flagged where live. And the author's synthesis, like the theories it judges, is now a belief in the world it describes, subject to Section 5.3's loop: it, too, should be scored on its posted forecasts.
